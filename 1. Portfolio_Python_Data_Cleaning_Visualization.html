An Analysis on the Hong Kong Information and Communications Industry¶

Author: Rowan Law¶

Date: 20 June 2017¶

Introduction¶

Hong Kong is well-known for its excellent infrastructure, stable power supply and relatively stable political environment. With the rise of the data-related industry, the Hong Kong Government is looking to promote the city as the regional hub for data centers. While at the same time, Hong Kong is also known for its lag in technological research and development.

With this in mind, we will look from the angle of employment in the Information and Communications (as "I&C" below) industry in Hong Kong. We will compare the employment evolution in the industry and that overall in Hong Kong from 2005 to 2015. We will also compare across Hong Kong and Japan to gain insights in the growth of the sector.

Data source¶

We will source the data from the government statistics bureaus in HK and Japan:

  1. HK Labour Force Data (source: Hong Kong Census and Statistics Department)
    • http://www.censtatd.gov.hk/hkstat/sub/sp200.jsp?tableID=006&ID=0&productType=8
  2. HK Information and Communication Sector Employment Data (source: Hong Kong Census and Statistics Department)
    • http://www.censtatd.gov.hk/hkstat/sub/sp350.jsp?tableID=091&ID=0&productType=8
  3. Japan Labour Force (source: Japan Statistics Bureau)
    • http://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/roudou/lngindex.htm
    • Historical Data 2
  4. Japan Information and Communication Sector Employment Data (source: Japan Statistics Bureau)
    • http://www.stat.go.jp/english/data/roudou/lngindex.htm
    • Historical Data 5 (1)

Chart 2¶

We then zoom into the I&C sector. We plot its population evolution, overlayed with the line charts for the changes in the overall population and the I&C sector population.

We notice two things:

  1. Contrast to what we see in the first chart. The change in the sector is actually sharper than than of the overall labour force. The trend is positive for the better amount of time.

  2. Both the sector and overall workforce undergo similar cyclicality. However, we also see that any change in the I&C sector always precedes that of the overall workforce. We may not be able to draw solid conclusion from this, but possible deduction can be that business would often cut back their IT expenditure first when they face hard times causing hardship in the relevant industry, or that the industry is pretty flexible and can cut staff when they halt development of products during hard times.

Chart 3¶

We compare the % of labour force engaged in the I&C sector in HK with that in Japan.

Although the I&C sector in HK is increasing in proportion, it is to an extent less significant than its counterpart in Japan. If the HK Government's goal is to promote HK as the regional hub of data center, they may find Japan, with its similar advantages in political environment and infrastructure, to be a substantial opponent.

Again we can see that in 2008 when the economic downturn was just starting, the I&C sector has quickly fallen to to its bottom. But the impact to the I&C sector was short-lived and the sector expanded soon after 2008.

Conclusion¶

With the limitation of the data, we cannot easily draw robust conclusion from the data. What is clearly visible is that the importance of the Information and Communications industry, in terms of workforce, has been increasing. However the Hong Kong society still does not place much emphasis on this sector, visible from the volatility of employed population in the industry.

Also when compared with the counterpart sector in Japan, Hong Kong is still lagging behind even after the recent years of expansion in this industry. Without the proper support from the labour market, the Hon Kong Government's target to set up the city as the regional hub for data centers would not be easily reached.
